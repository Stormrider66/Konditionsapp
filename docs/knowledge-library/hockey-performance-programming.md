# Ishockey Offseason Performance / Ice Hockey Offseason Performance Programming

## Skill Scope

This skill is a de-identified coaching knowledge base derived from a multi-year corpus of professional ice-hockey off-season programs, testing templates, rehabilitation notes, and phase plans from 2020-2026. It is weighted toward the newest 2024-2026 programming structure while preserving recurring patterns from earlier seasons.

Use this skill when the user asks for hockey-specific physical preparation, summer/off-season planning, return-to-skate preparation, acceleration, repeated sprint ability, anaerobic conditioning, hybrid hockey sessions, strength-power progression, stability/prehab work, or how to adapt training around ice sessions, travel, camp, injuries, or individual equipment constraints.

Do not expose athlete names, phone numbers, emails, personal notes, teams, or any private source details. Use the corpus only to extract coaching principles, session archetypes, and programming logic.

## Core Coaching Model

Elite hockey preparation in this system is built around a bridge from general physical capacity to on-ice expression:

1. Build the base: restore movement quality, trunk and hip control, aerobic/ergometer capacity, and robust lower-body strength.
2. Raise the ceiling: improve maximal force, high-output intervals, repeated sprint ability, and the ability to tolerate high mechanical and metabolic stress.
3. Convert to hockey expression: shift toward acceleration, speed, lateral power, reactive agility, short high-intensity work bouts, hybrid sessions, and ice-specific transfer.
4. Arrive fresh: reduce volume before camp, keep short high-quality speed/power touches, increase ice specificity, and avoid soreness-producing novelty.

The program should avoid treating hockey as pure endurance or pure strength. Hockey performance depends on repeated high-force actions under fatigue: acceleration, deceleration, changes of direction, contact strength, trunk stiffness, shoulder robustness, groin/hip capacity, and the ability to repeat hard shifts while recovering quickly.

## Annual and Off-Season Architecture

Most summer plans use a 10-14 week runway from May/June to late August or early September. The exact start depends on playoffs, national team duty, injury status, travel, and the athlete's report date.

### Phase 0: Intake and Testing Week

Purpose: establish readiness, individual constraints, baseline output, and summer priorities.

Common components:
- Short athlete interview: summer availability, travel, ice access, pain history, equipment access, team requirements, goals, and what the player wants to feel on the ice.
- Body and readiness screen: injury notes, hip/groin/shoulder/back/foot status, optional body composition if used by the staff.
- Aerobic or mixed-modal test: VO2 test, prolog, Wattbike/Assault/row/ski references, or another repeatable conditioning anchor.
- Neuromuscular test: CMJ, sprint timing/Freelap, acceleration, repeated sprint, power tests, or gym reference lifts.
- Output expectation: create a report or concise coaching summary directly after the test week, then use it to individualize phase one.

### Phase 1: Grund / Base and Rebuild

Typical duration: 3-4 weeks.

Primary goals:
- Rebuild training tolerance after the season.
- Restore trunk, hip, adductor, shoulder, and hamstring capacity.
- Establish technical quality in strength movements.
- Develop aerobic and A2/A3 capacity without excessive joint stress.
- Introduce acceleration mechanics, low-volume jumps, medicine-ball throws, or controlled agility if the athlete is ready.

Typical weekly structure:
- Monday: Strength 1, often lower-body strength with Kbox, unilateral work, sled acceleration, pull-ups/rows, trunk control.
- Tuesday: Conditioning, often A2/A3, 8x4 min, 10x3 min, prolog-based intervals, or full-body ergometer circuits.
- Wednesday: Stability/prehab plus low-intensity conditioning, upper body, mobility, or early agility.
- Thursday: Strength 2, often alternate bilateral/unilateral emphasis, squat/hinge/press, sprint-float-sprint if appropriate.
- Friday: High-intensity conditioning, hybrid, A3+, or repeated work capacity.
- Saturday: Optional extra low-intensity conditioning or individual work.
- Sunday: Off or very light recovery.

Phase 1 should feel productive but controlled. The player should not be crushed by soreness. Use this phase to build consistency, recover rhythm, and prepare the tissues for harder work.

### Phase 2: Max Strength and High-Capacity Transmutation

Typical duration: 3 weeks, often followed by a deload/lower week.

Primary goals:
- Increase maximal force and contact robustness.
- Progress Kbox, squat, split-squat, hinge, sled, pull-up, and pressing patterns.
- Convert base conditioning into higher repeatability and better output stability.
- Keep enough mobility/prehab frequency to protect groin, hip, shoulder, and hamstring tissue.

Progression principles:
- Reduce reps and increase intent/load compared with Phase 1.
- Use heavier sled accelerations with full recovery when speed quality matters.
- Use Kbox ISO holds and heavier bilateral/unilateral Kbox work when eccentric capacity and stiffness are priorities.
- Pair heavy lower-body work with explosive but low-volume jumps only if the athlete tolerates it.
- Use contrast only when technical quality and pain status allow it.

### Phase 3: Power, Speed, Agility, and Hockey-Specific Conversion

Typical duration: 3 weeks.

Primary goals:
- Convert strength to acceleration, power, lateral force, and short high-quality repeated efforts.
- Increase ice transfer through speed, reactive agility, lateral hops, sprint-float-sprint, short COD, and hockey-shift conditioning.
- Reduce unnecessary volume while preserving high intent.
- Integrate on-ice sessions as they increase from mid/late summer.

Common session changes:
- Strength becomes power-focused: fewer reps, higher velocity, medicine-ball throws, box jumps, Kbox explosive work, cleans or clean pulls when technically appropriate.
- Conditioning shifts from longer base intervals toward 3-2-1 patterns, E2MOM/E3MOM hybrids, 200 m repeats, repeated sprint patterns, and short work bouts with incomplete recovery.
- Agility becomes more reactive and less purely rehearsed.
- Ice work is treated as a high-value sport-specific stimulus, not just extra cardio.

### Phase 4: Camp Taper and Return-to-Season

Typical duration: 1-2 weeks.

Primary goals:
- Arrive at camp powerful and fresh.
- Keep short exposures to speed, strength, and high-intensity conditioning.
- Avoid large new exercises, heavy eccentric novelty, or high soreness risk.
- Let ice, team practice, travel, and testing dominate the final load decisions.

Reduce volume before reducing intensity. Keep brief accelerations, jumps, and high-quality lifts if readiness is good.

## Weekly Microcycle Templates

### Standard Five-Day Local Off-Season Week

Monday: Strength 1 plus acceleration or sled
- Warm-up 10 minutes easy ergometer or jog.
- Mobility/activation: Kang squat, lunge rotations, 90-90 hip bridge, sidelying hip work, floor press, shoulder rotation.
- Main lower-body strength: Kbox bilateral/unilateral, squat, split squat, or landmine skate squat.
- Secondary pull/press: weighted pull-ups, chest-supported row, push press or floor press.
- Hamstring/adductor/trunk: Flowin hamstring curl, bear crawl KB drag, rollout, Pallof press.

Tuesday: Conditioning
- A2/A3 base: 8x4 min with 2 min rest or 10x3 min with 1-2 min rest.
- Prolog model: 3 min maximal Wattbike/ergometer prolog, then intervals at 75-85 percent of prolog depending on phase.
- Full-body circuit option: row, run, ski erg, assault bike with controlled pacing.

Wednesday: Stability plus low-intensity or agility
- Hip/groin/shoulder/trunk block.
- Low-intensity 30-60 minutes or mixed ergometer volume.
- Add agility/jumps if the athlete is ready and the week needs neuromuscular work.

Thursday: Strength 2
- Alternate emphasis from Monday: bilateral if Monday was unilateral, unilateral if Monday was bilateral.
- Include sprint-float-sprint, lighter sled, or short accelerations if the athlete is not sore.
- Keep trunk stiffness and shoulder work.

Friday: High-intensity hybrid or A3+
- E2MOM/E3MOM mixed-modal blocks.
- Repeated sprint/hockey-shift intervals.
- Short high-output conditioning with enough rest to preserve quality.

Saturday/Sunday
- Optional extra low-intensity work, individual mobility, ice, or full rest.
- If the player has heavy ice, travel, or soreness, remove optional conditioning before removing quality strength/speed work.

### Three-to-Four-Day Semi-Supported Week

Use this when the athlete cannot be coached every day.

Day 1: Coached strength-power priority.
Day 2: Self-directed conditioning with clear targets and recording instructions.
Day 3: Coached stability/agility or second strength session.
Day 4: Self-directed hybrid, low-intensity conditioning, or ice-specific work.

Give the athlete exact recording instructions: watts, calories, split times, RPE, pain score, and whether output dropped across intervals.

## Strength and Power System

### Warm-Up Pattern

Use a warm-up that prepares hips, trunk, shoulders, and ankles before intensity:
- 5-10 min easy ergometer, bike, jog, row, or ski erg.
- Kang squat or OH squat for depth and posture.
- Lunge rotations or Cossack squat for hip rotation and groin exposure.
- 90-90 hip bridge, side-lift progressions, dog-on-bench, Bomben, Draken/RDL with rotation.
- Shoulder preparation: floor press, lying internal/external rotation, scapular complex, YTW, TRX T, or controlled press variations.
- Trunk: plank, side plank, deadbug, Pallof press, bear crawl patterns.

### Phase 1 Strength Menu

Purpose: establish tissue tolerance, positions, unilateral control, and basic force production.

Common main options:
- Kbox bilateral squat 3-4x5-8.
- Kbox unilateral/split squat 3-4x5 each side.
- Sled acceleration 6-8x10 m with sufficient rest.
- Squat or cyclist squat 3x5 plus back-off sets.
- Kickstand RDL or single-leg RDL 3-4x6-8.
- Landmine skate squat or lateral squat 3x6-8 each side.
- Weighted pull-ups or chins 3-4 sets.
- Push press, L-sit shoulder press, floor press, or single-arm press 3-4 sets.

The technical rule is simple: positions first, load second, speed third. Do not chase load if hip, knee, trunk, or shoulder line breaks.

### Phase 2 Max Strength Menu

Purpose: raise force ceiling and prepare the player for contact, acceleration, and late-summer intensity.

Common main options:
- Heavy Kbox ISO: 3 ramping holds of 3-5 seconds above 95 percent effort.
- Kbox unilateral max 5x3 each side.
- Kbox bilateral max 5x3.
- Heavy sled acceleration 4-6x10 m with full rest.
- Squat, split squat, trap bar deadlift, hip thrust, or Bulgarian split squat for 3-5 reps.
- Weighted pull-ups/chins 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps.
- Push press or heavy press 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps.

Use contrast pairings sparingly:
- Squat plus box jump.
- Split squat plus lateral hop.
- Trap bar or hip thrust plus jump.
- Medicine-ball throw plus chest/push pattern.

Contrast is a tool, not a default. It belongs when the athlete is pain-free, technically stable, and not overloaded by ice or conditioning.

### Phase 3 Power Menu

Purpose: convert force to speed, impulse, and hockey transfer.

Common options:
- Light sled acceleration 4x10 m at maximum intent.
- Sprint build-ups 3-5 reps.
- Sprint-float-sprint 4-6 reps of 5+5+10 m or similar.
- Kbox explosive unilateral 4x4 each side with lighter flywheel.
- Medicine-ball chest pass, scoop pass, rotational throw, or slam 3-5x3-6.
- Box jump, seated box jump, lateral bound, skater jump, sequence jumps, or low-volume plyometrics.
- KB swing, clean pull, hang power clean, trap bar jump, or band-assisted jump when technically appropriate.

Power work must be fresh. Stop sets when speed, landing quality, or trunk/hip control drops.

## Conditioning System

### A2 / A3 Base Conditioning

Use longer intervals to build repeatable output and recovery capacity:
- 8x4 min with 2 min rest.
- 10x3 min with 1-2 min rest.
- Full-body circuits using row, ski erg, assault bike, Wattbike, curve treadmill, running, or uphill walking.

Pacing rule: the athlete should finish feeling that 1-2 more intervals at the same output were possible. If watts, calories, or pace drop meaningfully between intervals, the first intervals were too hard.

Recording rule:
- Note watt, calories, split, heart rate, or distance for every interval.
- Use the same machine setup when comparing sessions.
- For bike checks, document fan/magnet/resistance and cadence settings if relevant.

### Prolog-Based Conditioning

Use when you need a clear athlete-specific target without a full lab test.

Protocol:
- Warm up 15 min.
- Perform 1x3 min at the highest sustainable output.
- Rest 10-15 min.
- Start follow-up work at 70-80 percent of prolog in early phases.
- Progress to 80-85 percent if output is stable and recovery is good.

Common follow-up examples:
- 6x3 min at 75 percent of prolog with 1-2 min rest.
- 8-10x3 min at 80 percent of prolog.
- 3x6 min at 85 percent of prolog for stronger athletes.

Use prolog work carefully if the athlete has accumulated ice load, groin symptoms, illness, or poor sleep.

### Hockey-Shift Conditioning and RSA

Hockey-specific conditioning must eventually include short work bouts, incomplete recovery, and high output:
- 3-2-1 min x 6 with 30 sec active rest between segments and 2 min between rounds.
- 15/45, 20/40, or 30/30 patterns for repeated sprint/agility circuits.
- 12 rounds E3MOM with bike/run or bike/curve components.
- 15-20x200 m with 1 min rest, or split into straight and shuttle/idiot-form variants.
- Repeated accelerations or 5-10-5/5-10-15-20 patterns with full technical intent.

The goal is not random suffering. The goal is repeatability: high outputs, controlled drop-off, and the feeling of being able to reproduce hard shifts late in a game.

### Hybrid Sessions

Hybrid Friday sessions combine ergometer, running, loaded carries/lunges, burpees, clean and jerk, push press, rows, sleds, or similar movements.

Example E2MOM structure:
- Block 1: 7 rounds of 250 m row plus 14 walking lunge steps.
- Rest 3 min.
- Block 2: 7 rounds of 25 cal assault bike plus 8-10 burpees.
- Rest 3 min.
- Block 3: 7 rounds of 200 m run plus 6 clean and jerk, with substitutions if foot/hip/shoulder status requires it.

Progress by increasing quality first, then load/reps. The athlete should usually feel they could complete one more round after the last one. If technique collapses, the session is too heavy or too dense.

## Agility, Speed, and Lateral Transfer

Agility sessions use a blend of rehearsed movement, reactive tasks, jumps, and short COD work.

Common structure:
1. Warm-up: jog or bike 7-15 min, then dynamic prep.
2. Low-volume jumps: skater jumps, sequence hops, single-leg forward/lateral hops, vertical-to-broad jump, lateral hurdle jumps.
3. COD and acceleration: 5-10-5, 5-10-15-20, T-run, X-drill, short sprint out of shuffle, sled push relay.
4. Reactive work: mirror shuffle, clap/number/direction reaction, cone touch with call-outs, reactive lateral hop.
5. Density block: 15/45, 20/40, or 3 rounds of station work when conditioning is also desired.

Agility should never be a junk-volume footwork circuit. Decide whether the goal is speed quality, reactive agility, conditioning, or tissue exposure. Rest accordingly.

## Stability, Prehab, and Return-to-Train Logic

The corpus repeatedly emphasizes shoulder, hip, groin, trunk, hamstring, and foot/ankle robustness. These are not accessories; they are part of the hockey performance system.

### Common Stability Menu

Hip/groin/adductor:
- 90-90 hip bridge and hip lift variations.
- Copenhagen/adductor plank progressions.
- Side-lift 1-step and 2-step.
- Cossack squat and lateral squat.
- Dog-on-bench, Bomben, Draken/RDL with flies or rotation.
- Groin squeeze, controlled lateral lunge, and low-volume skater patterns.

Trunk:
- Plank, side plank, side plank march.
- Deadbug with band from side.
- Pallof press and explosive Pallof variants.
- Bear crawl KB drag, bear crawl body saw, rollout.
- Cable wood chop, landmine rotation, anti-rotation rows.

Shoulder/upper body:
- Floor press with dumbbell.
- Lying internal/external rotation.
- Scapular complex, YTW, TRX T, kryssdrag.
- Chest-supported row, bird-dog row, one-arm row.
- Gradual push/press progression if shoulder history exists.

Hamstring/posterior chain:
- Flowin hamstring curl.
- Single-leg RDL and kickstand RDL.
- Hip thrust.
- Back extension/GHR with controlled pelvis.

### Injury and Pain Rules

When pain exists, first preserve rhythm and safe qualities. Modify the session before canceling all training:
- Foot pain: replace running with ski erg, row, assault bike, or Wattbike; remove jumps temporarily.
- Shoulder sensitivity: reduce vertical pressing, use horizontal push, floor press, controlled rotator cuff, rows, and gradual contact prep.
- Groin/adductor symptoms: lower lateral speed, keep isometrics and controlled adductor progressions, avoid high-volume skater jumps and aggressive COD until response is stable.
- Hamstring history: keep sprint exposure progressive, preserve RDL/hamstring curl quality, avoid fatigue-based sprinting until ready.

Stop or refer when pain is sharp, worsening, changes mechanics, produces limping, or persists after load reduction.

## Individualization Rules

Before generating a plan, clarify:
- Report date, camp date, and key testing dates.
- Current phase, weeks available, and expected ice schedule.
- Training age and recent season load.
- Pain/injury status: hip, groin, shoulder, back, hamstring, knee, foot/ankle.
- Equipment: Kbox/flywheel, sled, Wattbike, assault bike, ski erg, rower, curve treadmill, track, ice, Freelap/timing, medicine balls.
- Coaching model: full service, semi-supported, or remote.
- Athlete preference: strength, speed, conditioning, contact robustness, shoulder/groin rehab, on-ice transfer.

Position and athlete type adjustments:
- Larger defensemen/contact-heavy players often need contact strength, trunk stiffness, hip/adductor robustness, sled work, and controlled acceleration.
- Smaller forwards often need acceleration, repeated sprint quality, power-to-weight, and high-output repeatability.
- Returning injured athletes need more conservative phase transitions and clearer pain-response rules.
- Goalies need more hip/groin control, lateral power, trunk stiffness, mobility, and careful management of adductor/hip volume; do not overload repeated linear sprinting by default.
- Younger or first-year pro athletes need more technical teaching, less assumed training tolerance, and clearer session notes.

## Progression and Decision Rules

Progress only one major stressor at a time:
- Add load OR reps OR density OR speed OR complexity, not all at once.
- If adding ice volume, reduce off-ice conditioning or lower-body jump/COD volume first.
- If adding max strength, protect sleep, soreness, and next-day skating quality.
- If adding high-intensity intervals, keep the next day lower intensity or stability-focused.

Deload/lower weeks:
- Use every 3-4 weeks or when travel, ice volume, camp, or accumulated fatigue requires it.
- Reduce volume 30-50 percent, keep movement quality, keep short speed/strength touches if readiness is good.
- Deloads can be active: low-intensity conditioning, technique, mobility, trunk/hip work, and easy ice.

Readiness cues:
- Good sign: stable output across intervals, normal warm-up feel, good jump/sprint quality, soreness resolves in 24-48h.
- Yellow flag: output drop-off, heavy legs, lower jump quality, elevated RPE for normal loads, minor pain that changes movement.
- Red flag: sharp pain, limping, worsening groin/shoulder/back symptoms, illness, repeated sleep disruption, or inability to recover between sessions.

## AI Response Style for This Skill

When this skill is active, the AI should behave like a hockey performance coach:
- Give concrete phase logic, not generic workouts.
- Explain why each session exists in hockey terms.
- Use Swedish or English to match the user.
- Keep plans practical, with exact days, session goals, exercises, sets/reps, conditioning targets, substitutions, and recording instructions.
- If information is missing, make conservative assumptions and state them.
- If the request is unsafe or too aggressive, say so clearly and offer a safer adjustment.
- Never pretend that one template fits every player.

For generated plans, prefer this output structure:
1. Phase and goal.
2. Weekly structure.
3. Detailed sessions.
4. Progression for the next 2-4 weeks.
5. Monitoring/recording.
6. Adjustments for pain, ice, travel, or equipment.

## Example Plan Logic

If asked for a first 3-week base block for a healthy skater:
- Monday: Strength 1 with Kbox/squat, sled acceleration, pull, press, trunk.
- Tuesday: 8x4 min or 10x3 min conditioning with strict output recording.
- Wednesday: Stability plus low-intensity conditioning or controlled agility.
- Thursday: Strength 2 with unilateral emphasis, hinge, lateral squat, upper-body pull/press.
- Friday: A3+ or hybrid, but controlled enough to maintain repeatability.
- Weekend: optional A1/A2 conditioning, mobility, or ice.

If asked for late-summer conversion:
- Monday: Power strength with MB throws, low-volume jumps, explosive lower body.
- Tuesday: Speed/agility with full rest and reactive COD.
- Wednesday: low-intensity conditioning plus stability.
- Thursday: heavier but lower-volume strength or contrast work.
- Friday: hockey-shift conditioning/hybrid.
- Increase ice quality and reduce off-ice volume as camp approaches.

If asked for an athlete with shoulder history:
- Keep rotator cuff/scapular prep 2-3x/week.
- Start with floor press/horizontal push and rows before heavy overhead work.
- Avoid heavy clean and jerk, high-volume burpees, or aggressive contact circuits until shoulder response is stable.
- Use pain and next-day response to progress.

If asked for groin/hip robustness:
- Use 90-90, adductor plank progressions, side-lift, Cossack/lateral squat, controlled skater hops, and progressive lateral COD.
- Avoid jumping immediately to high-volume skater jumps or repeated aggressive changes of direction.

## Boundaries

This skill supports coaching and training design. It does not diagnose injuries, replace medical care, or override a club medical/performance staff plan. For pain, return-to-play decisions, concussion, acute injury, swelling, neurological symptoms, or persistent worsening symptoms, the AI must recommend professional evaluation and coach/medical review.
